📖 Consultative Sales Playbook
Account-specific strategies for every referral source type. Know how they think before you walk in.
🏥 Skilled Nursing Facilities (SNFs) ▼
🧠 How They Think
  • SNF administrators and DONs live and die by re-hospitalization rates and star ratings. CMS is watching them - they know it.
  • They're juggling census pressure, staffing shortages, and constant surveys. They don't have time for vendors who waste it.
  • Discharge planners want partners who accept patients fast, communicate clearly, and don't bounce patients back.
  • They've been burned by agencies that over-promise and under-deliver - trust is earned, not pitched.
❤️ What They Care About
  • Speed of intake and start of care
  • Reducing 30-day re-hospitalizations
  • Clear, proactive communication on patient progress
  • Helping them maintain or improve their star rating
  • Making their discharge process easier, not harder
😤 What Frustrates Them About Home Health
  • "We send a referral and don't hear back for 24+ hours."
  • "The agency accepted the patient and then never started care on time."
  • "We had a patient bounce back and the home health agency didn't even know about it."
  • "They show up, drop off brochures, and ask for referrals. That's not a partnership."
🔑 Probing Questions
  • "What does your current discharge-to-home workflow look like? Where do things typically get stuck?"
  • "When you send a referral to your home health partner, what does the first 24 hours look like? Are you getting the communication you need?"
  • "How are you tracking 30-day re-hospitalization rates for your post-discharge patients?"
  • "If I could show you a way to reduce your bounce-backs on cardiac and COPD patients specifically, would that be worth a conversation?"
💬 Opening Lines
"I'm not here to sell you on another home health agency. I want to understand your discharge challenges and see if the way we operate would actually solve a problem for you. Can I ask - what's the biggest headache in your discharge process right now?"
"I know you've got preferred providers. I'm not asking you to change anything today. I just want to learn how your team handles transitions to home - and if there's ever a gap, I'd like to be the name you call."
⭐ High-Value Questions
  • "What percentage of your discharges go home with home health vs. to another facility?"
  • "Who on your team is most involved in that home health decision - is it case management, the DON, or does it come from the medical director?"
  • "How do you evaluate whether a home health partner is actually performing for your patients?"
🤝 Closing for Partnership
"Here's what I'd love to do - let me take one or two of your next complex discharges and show you what our process looks like. You'll see our response time, our communication cadence, and our clinical follow-through. If it works, we keep going. If it doesn't, no hard feelings. Fair enough?"
🏋️ Inpatient Rehab Facilities (IRFs) ▼
🧠 How They Think
  • IRFs focus on functional outcomes and discharge to community - that's their quality metric.
  • They want patients to go home successfully, not bounce to a SNF or back to acute care.
  • Therapy teams are key influencers - they know exactly who's going home and what support they need.
  • They need home health to continue the rehab plan, not restart from scratch.
❤️ What They Care About
  • Continuity of therapy plan from inpatient to home
  • PT/OT/ST availability and quality
  • Speed - they need home health set up before the patient leaves
  • Communication loop back to the rehab physician
💬 Opening Lines
"I know your patients have worked hard to get functional gains in rehab. The last thing anyone wants is for that progress to stall at home. I'd love to understand how you're currently bridging that transition - and whether there are gaps we could help fill."
🔑 Probing Questions
  • "When a patient discharges home, how confident is your team that the home health agency is continuing the therapy plan you've built?"
  • "What's the feedback loop look like? Do you hear back on patient progress after discharge?"
  • "Have you had situations where a patient regressed because home therapy didn't pick up where you left off?"
🤝 Closing for Partnership
"What if we set up a direct communication line between your therapy team and ours? We can get the plan of care details before discharge and ensure seamless continuation. Let me show you how it works with one patient."
🏨 Hospitals (Acute Care) ▼
🧠 How They Think
  • Hospitals are measured on readmission penalties, length of stay, and patient satisfaction (HCAHPS).
  • Case managers are overwhelmed - they're handling 15-25 discharges a day and need reliable partners.
  • They want home health agencies that say yes, move fast, and don't create callbacks.
  • Value-based care contracts are pushing them to track post-acute outcomes more than ever.
❤️ What They Care About
  • Reducing 30-day readmissions (CMS penalties are real money)
  • Fast, reliable referral acceptance
  • Agencies that accept complex patients, not just the easy ones
  • Data they can use - patient outcomes reported back
  • HCAHPS impact - patients who go home well-supported rate the hospital higher
💬 Opening Lines
"I know your case management team doesn't need another agency asking for referrals. What they need is a partner who actually performs when patients get home. Can I ask - what's the biggest gap you're seeing in your post-discharge transitions right now?"
🔑 Probing Questions
  • "How are you currently tracking which home health agencies are actually reducing your readmission rates vs. just accepting referrals?"
  • "When a referral falls through or an agency can't staff a case - how often is that happening and what does it cost your team?"
  • "Are you seeing any specific diagnosis groups bouncing back more than others? CHF, COPD?"
🤝 Closing for Partnership
"Let's start small and prove it. Give us your next 5 complex cardiac or respiratory discharges. I'll personally track the outcomes and bring you a report in 30 days. If we perform, we expand. If we don't, you've lost nothing. Sound fair?"
👨‍⚕️ Physician Offices ▼
🧠 How They Think
  • Physicians think in terms of patient outcomes and liability. They want their patients cared for and their name protected.
  • Office staff (MAs, practice managers, referral coordinators) are the gatekeepers - they decide who gets access.
  • Most physicians don't fully understand what home health does or how it benefits their practice. Education is the play.
  • They want minimal friction - if referring to you creates more work for their office, they'll stop.
❤️ What They Care About
  • Patient compliance between visits
  • Reducing ER visits and hospital admissions for their panel
  • Getting clinical updates without chasing them
  • Easy, low-friction referral process
  • MIPS/quality measure performance (for value-based practices)
💬 Opening Lines
"Doctor, I'm not here to pitch you on home health. I'm here because I think we can help you keep your sicker patients - the ones you worry about between visits - out of the ER and on track with their plan of care. Can I show you how a few of your peers are using us?"
"Hi [Office Manager Name], I work with a few practices in the area who've found that having a home health partner reduces their patient callbacks and ER diversions. I'd love 5 minutes to show you how it works - no commitment, just information."
🤝 Closing for Partnership
"Can I leave you a simple one-page reference on when to refer to home health? And if a patient comes to mind - even one - let me show you how easy the referral process is. I'll handle everything from there."
🏡 Assisted Living / Senior Living ▼
🧠 How They Think
  • ALF directors think in terms of resident retention. Every hospital transfer is a potential move-out.
  • They are not clinical facilities - they rely on outside partners for skilled care.
  • Families pressure them: "Why wasn't this caught earlier?" Home health is a safety net.
  • They want partners who communicate, show up reliably, and don't disrupt their community.
❤️ What They Care About
  • Keeping residents in the community and out of the hospital
  • Family satisfaction - families want to know their loved one is being monitored
  • Fall prevention and chronic disease management
  • Reliable, professional clinicians who respect the community setting
💬 Opening Lines
"I know your number one priority is keeping your residents healthy and in the community. We specialize in helping communities like yours catch clinical changes early - things like CHF exacerbation, fall risk escalation, medication issues - before they become ER visits. Can I show you how we've helped similar communities?"
🤝 Closing for Partnership
"Here's what I'd suggest - let me come in and do a quick educational session for your team on when home health can step in vs. when it's an ER situation. No cost, no commitment. It helps your staff, and it gives you a chance to see how we operate. Would next week work?"
🧙‍♀️ Getting Past the Window Witch ▼
🧠 Who Is the Window Witch?
  • The Window Witch is the front desk gatekeeper - the receptionist, unit clerk, medical assistant, or office manager who stands between you and your decision-maker.
  • She (or he) didn't get that job by accident. They are paid to protect people's time. They are not the enemy - they are the test.
  • The reps who treat them like an obstacle get blocked forever. The reps who treat them like a VIP ally get waved through every time.
  • Remember: the Window Witch often has more influence over the case manager's perception of you than you realize. She talks. A lot.
🔑 The Core Principle
  • Make her feel seen, respected, and even a little special. She gets talked at all day by people who see her as a speed bump. You're going to be different.
  • Learn her name on the first visit. Use it every single time after that. It signals you're not just another rep churning through accounts.
  • Never show frustration if she says "she's not available." Thank her warmly, leave something thoughtful, and come back with the same energy.
  • The goal: make her want to tell the case manager, "That [your name] is really nice - she's been coming by again."
🏆 The Soft Skills That Actually Work
  • The compliment-and-remember combo: Notice something specific - her earrings, her desk photo, a decoration, something she mentioned last time. Bring it up next visit. "How did your daughter's soccer tournament go?" She will never forget you.
  • The food bribe (done right): Never bring food and immediately ask for access. Bring it, leave it at the desk, say "just wanted to brighten your day - no agenda today," and walk away. That builds more equity than 10 hard pitches.
  • The insider position: Ask for her advice. "Hey, is this a good time to try to catch [case manager name] or does she have a better time of day?" You're making her feel like your guide, not your obstacle.
  • The name card play: Leave a handwritten note with your card - not a generic card, a personal one. "Sarah - so great to see you today. Hope your week gets easier. -[Your name]" Two sentences. Massive impact.
  • The champion ask: After 3-4 warm visits, say: "Hey, I know [case manager] is slammed. Is there any chance you could let her know I stopped by and that I'd love just 5 minutes whenever she has a breath? I promise I won't waste her time." Now Sarah is your messenger.
🧊 For the Hardest-to-Crack Window Witches
  • The ice-cold front desk who won't even look up: Don't take it personally - she's seen 50 reps this week. Drop off something with a tiny, handwritten note, make zero ask, and leave with a warm "Have a great rest of your day." Consistency over weeks will thaw even the frostiest gatekeeper.
  • The "She's in a meeting" loop: She's always in a meeting. Change your time. Come early morning (7:30–8am), right after lunch (1–1:30pm), or late afternoon (4–4:30pm). Ask: "Is there a time she's generally more reachable?" Most will tell you.
  • The policy blocker ("No reps allowed without an appointment"): Respect the policy. Ask: "Totally understood - how do I get on her calendar? Is there an email or a scheduling process?" Then follow it perfectly. Then show up with coffee on the day of your appointment like you own the world.
  • The silent treatment: Some gatekeepers will literally not engage no matter what. Go around - politely. Network at a health fair or community event where you can meet the case manager organically. Or find her on LinkedIn and send a thoughtful message. Then when you come in, you already have a connection she didn't give you.
  • The unhinged nuclear option (for truly impenetrable accounts): Show up at an inservice, a facility event, or a community education session where the gatekeeper's job doesn't apply. Now you're presenting value in a room where she can't block you. Once you're in front of the decision-maker, let your expertise do the rest.
💬 Exact Language That Works
"Hi [name]! Good to see you again. I'm not here to be a pain - I know she's slammed. I just wanted to drop this off and say hi. You doing okay today?"
"I know this is a crazy time of day. Could I just leave this for [case manager name] and maybe ask you - is there a time of week she's a little more reachable? I want to respect her schedule."
"Hey, you've been so helpful every time I come in. I genuinely appreciate it. When you see her, would you mind just mentioning I stopped by? No pressure at all - I just want her to know I'm consistent."
⚡ The Golden Rule
  • Treat the Window Witch like she's the one giving you the referral. Because in many ways - she is. She controls access, she shapes perception, and she can turn a "she's not available" into "let me go grab her real quick."
  • Patience + consistency + genuine warmth = access. No hack replaces showing up and caring.
🎋 Bamboo Pings - Using Real-Time Alerts to Your Advantage ▼
🧠 What Is Bamboo Health (PatientPing)?
  • Bamboo Health (formerly PatientPing) is a real-time care coordination platform used by hospitals, health systems, health plans, and post-acute providers across the country.
  • When a patient is admitted to an ER, admitted to a hospital, discharged, or transferred - Bamboo sends an instant notification called a "Ping" to every care team member enrolled in the network for that patient.
  • This means case managers, care coordinators, and providers get a real-time alert the moment something happens with their patient - no chasing charts, no waiting on a phone call.
  • For home health sales reps: Pings are one of the most powerful tools in your arsenal - but only if you know how to use them correctly.
🎯 How to Use Bamboo Pings to Your Advantage
  • Proactive outreach on admissions: When you receive a Ping that a patient from one of your referring accounts has been admitted to the hospital, reach out to that case manager before she reaches out to you. You're showing her you're watching - and that you're already on it.
  • Same-day discharge coordination: A discharge Ping gives you the earliest possible heads-up that a patient is transitioning home. Use it to initiate intake faster than any competitor could. Speed here is the ultimate differentiator.
  • Prevent re-hospitalizations in real time: If a patient you're currently serving pings an ER admission, your team can coordinate immediately to prevent a full hospitalization. This is genuine value you can prove - and report back to the case manager.
  • Build your case with data: Keep a log of Pings you've acted on and outcomes you've influenced. This becomes your proof-of-performance story when you're sitting across from a case manager or discharge planner.
💬 How to Position Bamboo Pings to Case Managers
  • Case managers are drowning. They're handling 15-25 discharges a day and worrying about the patients who already went home. Bamboo Pings let you be their safety net without them having to ask.
  • When you reach out using a Ping-triggered insight, frame it as partnership intelligence - not as surveillance. "I saw that [patient] had an ER visit last night - I wanted to make sure our team has already coordinated and that you're aware we're on it."
  • This creates a very specific feeling in the case manager's mind: this agency is paying attention, they're proactive, and they're reducing my work. That's the feeling that earns referrals.
🗣️ Language That Makes Case Managers Believe You're Adding Real Value
"Hey [name], I wanted to reach out because I saw through our care coordination platform that [patient name] had an ER event last night. Our team is already in contact and I wanted to make sure you had visibility on that - and to let you know we're coordinating to keep this from turning into a full readmission. You don't need to do anything on your end - we're on it."
"One of the things that differentiates how we operate is that we use Bamboo Health alerts so our team knows the moment one of your patients has a care event. It means we're never reactive - we're always ahead of it. That's how we keep your readmission numbers down."
"I know you don't have time to follow up on every patient who walks out that door. We do that for you. When we get a Ping, we call. We coordinate. We document. And then we report back to you. We're basically your eyes and ears after discharge."
⚡ The Patient Ping Sales System
  • Step 1 - Get set up: Make sure your agency is enrolled in Bamboo Health and that you understand how to read and act on Pings for your patient panel.
  • Step 2 - Build a response protocol: When a Ping comes in, there should be a clear internal process - who calls the patient, who calls the case manager, who documents. Your speed and consistency is what gets noticed.
  • Step 3 - Create touchpoints: Every Ping you act on is a reason to contact the referring case manager. These aren't cold calls - they're warm, value-added check-ins. Over time, this pattern trains case managers to associate you with reliability.
  • Step 4 - Close with data: At your next visit, bring a brief summary: "Since we started working together, we've intercepted 3 potential readmissions using real-time alerts. Here's what happened with each one." That's a conversation no competitor is having with her.
  • Step 5 - Make it their idea: After a few months of consistent Ping-driven outreach, ask: "Has it been helpful that we've been reaching out proactively when patients have care events? That's something we try to do for all our partners - and I want to make sure it's actually useful for your team." She will say yes. And now she's sold herself.
🧭 Emotional Intelligence in Sales - The Consultative↔Relational Scale ▼
🧠 The Core Concept
  • The best sales reps don't have one mode. They have a dial - and they know how to read the room and adjust it in real time.
  • On one end of the dial: Consultative - data-driven, analytical, outcome-focused. You're solving a problem. You're speaking in metrics, outcomes, and clinical language.
  • On the other end: Relational - warm, personal, emotionally attuned. You're building a human connection. You're seeing the person, not just the referral source.
  • Neither end is "better." The skill is knowing who you're in front of, what they need right now, and how to flex.

🔵 Consultative Mode - When & How
  • When to use it: You're with someone who responds to data, outcomes, and efficiency. They're analytical, time-pressed, and skeptical. They've heard the pitch before and need proof, not warmth.
  • How it sounds: Lead with outcomes and metrics. "Our 30-day readmission rate is X%. Here's how that compares to the market average. Here's what it means for your quality scores." Be concise, be credible, be prepared.
  • What to avoid: Don't overshare personal stories. Don't linger. Don't make small talk when they've signaled they want to get to the point. Respect their time visibly - "I'll keep this brief."
  • Watch for cues they're in consultative mode: They glance at their watch. They ask process-oriented questions. They cut you off to get to the point. They reference data or quality metrics unprompted.
"I know you're data-driven, so let me skip the pitch and get to the numbers. Here's what we've delivered for facilities like yours over the last 90 days - and here's exactly how we track it."
🟢 Relational Mode - When & How
  • When to use it: You're with someone who connects through people, stories, and trust. They need to like you and believe you care before they'll ever send a referral. Logic will not move them - relationship will.
  • How it sounds: Ask about them first. Listen more than you talk. Bring up something personal from your last conversation. Celebrate their wins. Commiserate with their frustrations. Be a human first, a sales rep second.
  • What to avoid: Don't show up with a data sheet. Don't rush to the ask. Don't check your phone. Give them your full, unhurried presence.
  • Watch for cues they're in relational mode: They ask how you're doing and actually wait for your answer. They tell you about their weekend. They have photos on their desk and point to them. They laugh easily and lean in.
"Before I talk about anything work-related - how are YOU doing? Last time you mentioned things were really slammed. Did it ever slow down?"
🎯 How to Read the Room Quickly
  • The first 30 seconds tell you everything. Did they look up? Did they smile? Did they make eye contact? Or did they stay heads-down and give you a distracted "yeah, what do you need?"
  • Match their energy first. If they're in a rush - be brisk, be efficient, honor their time. If they're relaxed and chatty - slow down, let the conversation breathe. Energy matching builds instant rapport.
  • Ask one open question and observe what they do with it. "How's your week been?" A relational person will run with it. A consultative person will answer in two words and pivot to business. Follow their lead.
  • Pay attention to their desk: Data charts and printouts = consultative. Family photos and plants = relational. Both = you have some flexibility.
🔄 Blending Both - The Advanced Move
  • The truly elite reps don't live at either extreme. They open with warmth (relational) to build safety, then pivot to value (consultative) to build credibility - then close with care (relational) to build trust.
  • Formula: "How are you?" → [listen] → "Here's something I thought would actually be useful for your team." → [share one insight] → "Is that something that would help, or is there something else on your radar right now?"
  • The goal: leave every conversation with the person feeling seen as a human AND confident you're a competent partner. That combination is rare. Reps who nail it are almost impossible to displace.
🚨 The Friend Zone Problem - And How to Climb Out
  • What the friend zone looks like: She lights up when you walk in. You talk about her kids, her weekend, her drama with the new director. She genuinely likes you. And she sends you zero referrals - or sends the same token few she always has. You're her favorite visitor. You are not her partner.
  • Why it happens: You leaned too hard into relational mode and never established yourself as a clinical resource and business asset. In her mind, you're the friendly rep who brings coffee - not the expert she relies on for complex discharges. The relationship is real. The professional credibility isn't there yet.
  • The hard truth: Being liked is not the same as being trusted professionally. She needs to believe you solve problems, not just make her day nicer. The friend zone is comfortable - for both of you. Breaking out of it requires intentional recalibration.
🔧 How to Recalibrate Without Killing the Relationship
  • Don't go cold - go strategic. The warmth is an asset. You're not abandoning it. You're layering credibility on top of it. Keep the personal connection, but start showing up with substance every single visit.
  • Introduce a business moment into every visit. After the personal catch-up, pivot cleanly: "Hey, while I have you - I actually wanted to show you something. We had a really interesting outcome with a patient last month that reminded me of some of the cases you mentioned struggling with." Now you're adding value, not just visiting.
  • Bring a specific insight, not a generic pitch. Generic: "We accept complex patients." Specific: "I pulled our outcomes data for CHF patients in the last 90 days - our 30-day readmit rate is 8.2%. The national benchmark is 21%. I thought you'd want to see that given how many cardiac discharges you're managing." That's not a sales call. That's a clinical conversation.
  • Ask a direct, professional question. At some point, you have to name what you want - warmly but clearly. "I feel like we have a genuinely great relationship and I love coming in here. I also want to make sure I'm actually being useful to your team on the clinical side. When you have a complex case you're not sure how to place - am I on the list of people you think to call?" The answer will tell you everything.
  • Create a trial moment: "I know I've been building this relationship for a while - would you be willing to try us on your next tough case? A complex wound, a high-fall-risk patient, something your current partners push back on? Let me show you what we actually do." Low barrier, concrete ask, high-proof opportunity.
💬 Language to Shift the Dynamic
"I love our relationship - genuinely. And I want to make sure I'm bringing you actual value, not just showing up and taking up your time. I've been doing some thinking and I want to ask you directly: what would it take for me to be the first person you call on a tricky discharge?"
"I feel like I've been showing up here as a friendly face, and I want to make sure I'm also showing up as a resource. Can I share one thing with you today that I think could actually make your life easier? It'll take two minutes."
"Hey, I want to be honest with you because I value this relationship. I've been coming in for a while and I want to earn more of your referrals - not by bugging you, but by actually being the best option for your patients. What's one thing I could do or show you that would make that click for you?"
⚡ The Friend Zone Escape Rules
  • Never abandon the warmth - that relationship equity is real and hard to rebuild if you lose it. The goal is to add credibility on top, not replace one with the other.
  • Be patient but be persistent. Recalibration takes a few visits. Don't try to cram 6 months of consultative positioning into one visit. Introduce one substantive element per conversation and build from there.
  • If the referrals still don't come after the recalibration, ask the hard question: "I want to ask you something and I hope it's okay - is there something about what we do or how we operate that's giving you pause? I'd rather know and address it than keep guessing." That conversation might be uncomfortable. It might also be the one that finally moves the needle.
  • Know when to accept the limit. Some accounts will always like you and never send you meaningful volume. That's data. Maintain the relationship, but don't over-invest time that should go to accounts where the path to referrals is real.
🛡️ Objection Handling
Every objection is information. Master the response, regain control, and advance the conversation.
🔍
💬 "We already have preferred providers."
▼
🧠 WHY THEY'RE REALLY SAYING IT
They're comfortable. The current provider is "good enough" and switching feels risky. They may also be telling you they don't see enough differentiation to justify a change. Sometimes it's a gatekeeper brush-off - they just want you to leave.
🚫 WHAT NOT TO SAY
"We're better than them." / "Who are you using? Let me tell you why we're different." / Anything that puts down the competitor. It makes you look desperate and disrespectful of their judgment.
✅ BEST RESPONSE
"That's great - and I'd never ask you to change something that's working. What I've found is that most facilities benefit from having a strong backup option, especially when census is high or a provider can't staff a case. I'm not looking to replace anyone - I just want to be the name you call when there's a gap. Would it be worth exploring what that looks like?"
🔄 FOLLOW-UP QUESTION TO REGAIN CONTROL
"When was the last time your current provider couldn't take a referral or had a staffing challenge? What happened?" - This gets them thinking about the gap without you pointing it out.
💬 "We rotate agencies."
▼
🧠 WHY THEY'RE REALLY SAYING IT
They want to appear fair and avoid committing. But rotation usually means no one agency is earning their loyalty - it's a sign of mediocrity across the board. This is actually an opportunity.
🚫 WHAT NOT TO SAY
"Why would you rotate when you could have one great partner?" / Don't challenge their system directly - they set it up and they'll defend it.
✅ BEST RESPONSE
"Totally understand - a lot of facilities do that. Quick question though: in your rotation, do you track which agency actually has the best outcomes? Lowest re-hospitalization rates, fastest start of care, best patient feedback? Because what I've seen is that facilities who start measuring performance end up naturally gravitating toward the partner who performs. I'd love the chance to be in that rotation and let the results speak."
🔄 FOLLOW-UP QUESTION
"If one agency in your rotation consistently outperformed the others, would that change how you allocate referrals?"
💬 "We're too busy right now."
▼
🧠 WHY THEY'RE REALLY SAYING IT
They probably ARE busy - respect that. But "busy" is also the easiest objection to throw out. The real question is whether they see enough value to give you even 60 seconds. You need to earn the right to their time by showing you respect it.
🚫 WHAT NOT TO SAY
"It'll just take a minute." / "When would be a better time?" (without adding value first) / Don't be the rep who doesn't take the hint.
✅ BEST RESPONSE
"I can see that - I'll be quick. I'm not here to take up your time. I just wanted to drop off something that might actually save you time on your discharge process. 🔄 Consultative Sales Framework
A repeatable, stage-by-stage process for winning partnerships - not just pitching services.
1
Pre-Call Planning
Win the visit before you walk in.
▼

Every great call starts before you arrive. The 5 minutes you spend planning are worth more than 30 minutes of winging it. Your goal: walk in with a purpose, not a brochure.

✅ Bulletproof Language
Before every call, answer three questions: "What do I know about this account?" "What is my specific objective for this visit?" "What value am I bringing that they can't get from anyone else?"

✅ DO

  • Review last visit notes before walking in
  • Check referral report for recent activity
  • Know the decision-maker's name and role
  • Have a specific ask ready (not "just checking in")
  • Bring something of value - data, education, a program overview

🚫 DON'T

  • Walk in without a plan
  • Say "I was in the area and thought I'd stop by"
  • Visit just to drop off donuts with no purpose
  • Assume you know what's changed since last visit
  • Skip pre-call because you're "familiar" with the account
💬 Example Dialogue
Internal prep: "Last time I visited Sunrise SNF, Sarah (the discharge planner) mentioned they were having issues with late start of care from their current HHA. She also had a complex cardiac patient going home soon."
My objective today: "Ask Sarah about that cardiac patient, position RightPath Cardiac, and get a trial referral."
My value-add: "I'm bringing our cardiac outcomes one-pager and offering to personally oversee the case."
2
Opening the Conversation
Earn the right to the conversation.
▼

The first 30 seconds set the tone. If you open with a pitch, you've already lost. Open with curiosity, not credentials.

✅ Bulletproof Language
"I'm not here to pitch - I'm here to listen. I want to understand what's working in your discharge process and where the friction is. If there's a way we can help, great. If not, at least I'll know how to stay out of your way."

✅ DO

  • Start with a question, not a statement
  • Reference something specific to their world
  • Be warm, direct, and respectful of time
  • Show you did your homework
  • Match their energy - if they're rushed, be brief

🚫 DON'T

  • Lead with your company name and services
  • Ask "Do you use home health?" (lazy)
  • Hand over a brochure as your opening move
  • Talk about yourself before asking about them
  • Ignore body language (if they're busy, offer to come back)
💬 Example Dialogue
You: "Hey Sarah - good to see you. Last time we talked, you mentioned you were dealing with some late starts of care from your home health partner. Has that gotten any better, or is it still creating headaches?"
Sarah: "Honestly, it's still an issue. We had one last week where the patient waited 3 days."
You: "Three days? That's tough - especially if it's a post-surgical or cardiac patient. Tell me more about what happened..."
3
Discovery Questions
Ask better questions than anyone else.
▼

Discovery is where you earn trust. The goal isn't to qualify them - it's to understand them so deeply that your solution feels like the obvious answer. The best reps ask the questions no one else is asking.

⭐ High-Impact Discovery Questions
  • "Walk me through what happens when you have a patient who needs home health - from the moment the order is written to the moment the agency starts care. Where does it break down?"
  • "How do you measure whether your home health partner is actually performing? Or is it more of a gut feeling?"
  • "When a patient bounces back within 30 days, what's the internal conversation like? Who feels that pressure?"
  • "If you could change one thing about how your home health partners communicate with you, what would it be?"
  • "What's keeping you up at night right now when it comes to patient transitions?"

✅ DO

  • Ask open-ended questions that start with "how" and "what"
  • Listen 80%, talk 20%
  • Take notes (shows you care)
  • Mirror back what they say to confirm understanding
  • Ask follow-up questions that go deeper

🚫 DON'T

  • Ask yes/no questions that kill momentum
  • Interrupt to pitch your solution mid-answer
  • Ask questions you could have answered with basic research
  • Make it feel like an interrogation
  • Rush through discovery to get to your pitch
4
Value Positioning
Position your value against their specific pain.
▼

Don't pitch features. Map your capabilities directly to the pain points they just told you about. If they said "our biggest issue is communication," your response better lead with communication - not your star rating.

✅ Bulletproof Language
"Based on what you're telling me, it sounds like the core issue is [THEIR PAIN POINT]. Here's specifically how we address that: [ONE SPECIFIC CAPABILITY]. And I can prove it - let me show you what that looked like with [SIMILAR ACCOUNT/CASE STUDY]."

✅ DO

  • Connect every feature to their stated problem
  • Use their words back to them
  • Share a relevant success story or data point
  • Keep it focused - 1-2 key differentiators max
  • Position programs (RightPath) as solutions, not products

🚫 DON'T

  • Feature-dump everything your agency does
  • Compare yourself to competitors by name
  • Use jargon they don't care about
  • Skip the "so what?" - always explain why it matters to THEM
  • Overpromise outcomes you can't guarantee
5
Handling Resistance
Resistance is a conversation, not a rejection.
▼

When someone pushes back, they're engaged - that's better than indifference. The formula: Acknowledge → Clarify → Reframe → Advance.

🔄 The A.C.R.A. Framework
  • Acknowledge: "I hear you, and that's a fair point."
  • Clarify: "Help me understand - is it [specific concern] or something else?"
  • Reframe: "What if we looked at it this way..." (offer a new perspective)
  • Advance: "Would it make sense to try [small, low-risk next step]?"

✅ DO

  • Stay calm and curious
  • Treat every objection as useful information
  • Ask follow-up questions to understand root cause
  • Offer a low-risk trial or next step
  • Know when to walk away and come back stronger

🚫 DON'T

  • Get defensive or argumentative
  • Dismiss their concern
  • Immediately counter with a rebuttal
  • Take it personally
  • Push harder when they're clearly done

Closing / Next Step
Always leave with a clear next step. Always.
▼

In consultative sales, the "close" is rarely a hard ask. It's a natural progression. But you MUST leave every interaction with a defined next step - otherwise, you're just having a nice conversation that leads nowhere.

✅ Soft Close Language That Works
"Based on everything we talked about, here's what I'd suggest as a next step: let me take your next [complex discharge / cardiac patient / post-surgical referral] and personally oversee it. You'll see our response time, our communication, and our outcomes on one real case. If it works, we go from there. Fair enough?"
"I don't need you to change anything today. What I'd love is just the chance to be your backup when the primary agency can't take a case. When that happens - and it will - call me, and let me show you how we respond."
"Would it make sense for me to set up a quick 15-minute educational session for your discharge team on [fall prevention / cardiac transitions / COPD management]? No pitch - just clinical value. That way your team gets something useful and I earn the right to a deeper conversation."

✅ DO

  • Propose a specific, low-commitment next step
  • Set a date and time - "Can I follow up Thursday?"
  • Summarize what you agreed on before leaving
  • Send a follow-up email within 2 hours
  • Log the interaction and action items immediately

🚫 DON'T

  • Leave without a defined next step
  • Say "I'll follow up soon" (vague = dead)
  • Ask "Can you start sending us referrals?" (too direct, too early)
  • Forget to follow up on what you promised
  • End with "Let me know if you need anything" (passive)

👑 Building an Empire
How to build a full book of business - your market, your territory, your empire - from zero to dominant.
🗺️ Phase 1 - Map Your Empire ▼
🧠 The Mindset Shift
  • Stop thinking about accounts. Start thinking about a territory - a living ecosystem of hospitals, SNFs, physicians, ALFs, and community partners that you are building to serve your agency.
  • Empire builders don't react to whatever walks in the door. They are deliberate, strategic, and relentless about constructing their market presence brick by brick.
  • The goal is not just referrals. The goal is market dominance - where your name is the first name that comes up every time someone in your territory needs home health.
📋 Territory Mapping Exercise
  • Step 1 - List every referral source type in your geography: Hospitals (acute care, critical access), SNFs, IRFs, ALFs, physician offices (primary care, cardiology, pulmonology, oncology, nephrology), home health agencies (competitors you might pull from), community organizations.
  • Step 2 - Research volume: Which hospitals have the highest discharge volume? Which SNFs have the highest census? Which physician practices have the largest Medicare/Medicaid patient panels? These are your highest-leverage targets.
  • Step 3 - Identify the white space: Where is your agency underrepresented? Where are competitors weak? Where is there a facility sending to an agency that's underperforming? Those are your entry points.
  • Step 4 - Build your master account list: Every account in your territory, organized by type and potential. This is your empire on paper. Now go build it in real life.
🏆 The Account Tiering System
TIER A - CROWN JEWELS
High volume, high relationship potential. These accounts can make your quarter alone. Visit weekly minimum. Know every name. Bring value every time.
TIER B - GROWTH ACCOUNTS
Moderate volume, active relationship. They send some - your job is to earn more. Visit every 2 weeks. Look for the opening to move them to Tier A.
TIER C - SEEDS
Low or no volume yet. These are your future empire. Visit monthly. Plant seeds. Stay consistent. They will bloom.
  • Review and re-tier your accounts monthly. An account's tier should reflect current reality - not where you hope they'll be.
  • The goal: move accounts up. Convert C's to B's, B's to A's. Over 12 months, a well-worked territory should have a growing Tier A base and a shrinking list of cold accounts.
🏗️ Phase 2 - Build the Foundation (Months 1–3) ▼
📍 The First 90 Days Blueprint
  • Month 1 - Discover and plant: Your only job is to show up everywhere and be memorable for the right reasons. Don't ask for referrals yet. Ask great questions. Learn their world. Take notes. Come back. Repeat.
  • Month 2 - Create value, not transactions: Start bringing insights, education, and genuine help. Host a lunch-and-learn. Drop off outcome data. Solve a problem you heard about in Month 1. Show them you listened.
  • Month 3 - Earn the first referral: By now you've visited enough times, built enough trust, and demonstrated enough value that the ask feels natural. "I'd love the chance to prove ourselves on one case - who do you have coming up that you're not sure how to place?" That's your first win. Nail it.
🔄 The Relationship Infrastructure
  • Know every decision-maker AND every influencer in each account. In a hospital, that might be 4-5 people: the case management director, individual case managers, social workers, unit nurses, and the discharge planning coordinator.
  • Map your contacts: For each account, document who you know, what you know about them personally, when you last visited, and what the status of the relationship is. Never rely on memory for a territory you're building.
  • Diversify your relationship portfolio: Reps who know only one person at each account are one resignation away from losing the account. Build depth - multiple contacts, multiple relationships, multiple champions.
  • Feed the network: Introduce contacts to each other when it makes sense. Refer them to resources. Be the connector in your market, not just a vendor showing up for referrals.
💡 The Content That Opens Doors
  • Lunch-and-learns on topics they care about (not your agency pitch): reducing readmissions, CHF management, fall risk protocols, transitional care best practices.
  • Outcome reports specific to the patients you've served from their facility - proof that you do what you say.
  • Relevant articles, CMS updates, or quality measure changes that affect their work. Be the one who keeps them informed.
  • Recognition moments: shout out a case manager who went above and beyond, or bring a "team of the month" treat to the nursing floor. People remember how you made them feel.
📈 Phase 3 - Grow the Empire (Months 4–9) ▼
🚀 Converting Accounts from Cold to Producing
  • The conversion ladder: Awareness → Familiarity → Trust → Trial → Loyalty. Every account is on this ladder. Your job is to identify where they are and pull them to the next rung.
  • Awareness: They know your name and your agency. Nothing more. Keep showing up. Keep dropping value. Don't rush.
  • Familiarity: They recognize you, smile when you walk in, know what you do. Now is the time to deepen the conversation - ask about their challenges, share specific solutions.
  • Trust: They believe you'll do what you say. This is earned through consistency, follow-through, and time. One broken promise here can reset the ladder. Honor every commitment, no matter how small.
  • Trial: They take a chance on you with one patient. This is the most critical moment in the whole journey. Execute flawlessly. Communicate proactively. Report back on outcomes. Make the experience so frictionless they can't imagine why they waited.
  • Loyalty: They're a producing account. Now your job shifts from winning them to keeping them - and growing the share.
📊 The Share-of-Wallet Strategy
  • Most facilities use multiple home health agencies. Your goal is to increase your share of their referrals over time - not just get in the door, but grow your slice.
  • Ask the question directly: "Of all the home health referrals you send in a given month, roughly what percentage comes our way?" If it's less than 50%, that's your growth target.
  • Identify the share blockers: Who else are they using? What are they getting from that agency that they don't think they're getting from you? Sometimes it's a relationship - sometimes it's a clinical capability. Find out and address it.
  • Grow share by outperforming: Speed of intake. Complexity acceptance. Communication quality. Outcome reporting. When you consistently outperform on these, the share follows. Let the results do the convincing.
🤝 Building a Referral Network That Feeds Itself
  • The web, not the funnel: An empire isn't a straight line from rep to referral. It's a web - where case managers refer patients, physicians trust your clinical team, SNFs see your readmission data, and families request you by name. Build the web.
  • Cross-referral relationships: When a SNF case manager mentions she has a patient heading to the hospital, offer to connect her with the hospital discharge team. When a physician needs a SNF recommendation, give him one (thoughtfully). Being the connector in your market builds reciprocal loyalty.
  • Create champions: Every empire has a handful of people who will go to bat for you internally - the case manager who tells her new colleague "call [your name]," the DON who recommends you in a meeting you're not in. Identify these people, invest in them, and never take them for granted.
🛡️ Phase 4 - Protect & Dominate (Month 9+) ▼
🔒 Protecting Your Book of Business
  • Never get complacent with your best accounts. The rep who stops showing up once they have the referrals is the rep who loses them to someone hungrier. Treat your Tier A accounts like you're still trying to win them.
  • Watch for warning signs: Referral volume drops without explanation. Your contact is suddenly "unavailable." A new agency rep is being seen at the account. These are signals - address them immediately, not after the account is lost.
  • The loyalty conversation: Once a year minimum, sit down with your top accounts and have a real conversation: "How are we doing? Is there anything we could be doing better or differently for your team?" Most reps never ask. The ones who do get to fix problems before they become defections.
  • Celebrate their wins publicly: When their facility hits a quality milestone, send a congratulations. When their case manager gets promoted, acknowledge it. When they hit a low-readmission quarter, credit them. People stay loyal to people who make them feel valued.
⚔️ Competing to Win - Taking Accounts from Competitors
  • Never attack a competitor directly. It looks unprofessional and makes the referral source feel like their judgment was wrong. Instead, let gaps surface naturally through smart questions.
  • "What's been your experience with the agencies you're currently using? Is there anything that's been inconsistent or frustrating?" - Let them tell you the problem. Then position your solution.
  • The Trojan Horse play: Get in the door as a backup for complex or overflowed cases. Nail every single one. Once they've seen you perform on the hard cases, the rotation starts shifting in your favor organically.
  • Time your moves around competitor failures: When a competitor drops a ball - a missed start of care, a communication breakdown, a readmission - that's your window. Not to kick them while they're down, but to quietly and professionally be the reliable option that's already there.
👑 What Empire Domination Looks Like
  • Your name comes up in conversations you're not in. Other reps hear about you. Facilities ask about you by name.
  • Your Tier A accounts have stayed with you through staff changes, leadership transitions, and new competitor reps. Loyalty survived because the relationship did.
  • You have multiple champions at multiple accounts who actively advocate for you. Your empire isn't held together by you alone - it's held together by a network of people who believe in you and your agency.
  • New accounts come to you through referrals from existing accounts. The network feeds itself.
  • You are not a vendor in your market. You are the standard.
📅 The Empire Builder's Weekly Rhythm
  • Monday: Review your full account list. Who hasn't heard from you in 2+ weeks? Who had a referral last week that needs a follow-up thank you? Set the week's priorities.
  • Tuesday–Thursday: Execution mode. Face-to-face visits, prioritized by tier. Tier A accounts get your best hours - mornings, when decision-makers are fresh and available.
  • Friday: Follow-through and future planning. Send the follow-up emails. CRM updates. Plan next week's route. Document anything new you learned about accounts this week.
  • Monthly: Re-tier your accounts based on what's actually happening. Celebrate wins with your team. Identify the one or two accounts you're going to focus on converting next month.
  • Quarterly: Step back and look at the whole empire. Where are you dominant? Where are you weak? What's your 90-day offensive plan?
